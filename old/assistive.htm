Featured Publication

Peer-Reviewed ACM IMWUT 2018 Wearable Computing Assistive Technology

FingerReader2.0: Designing a Wearable Reading Assistant for People with Visual Impairments

R. Boldu, S. Jain, A. Dancu, P. Maes, S. Zhao
Proceedings of the ACM on Interactive, Mobile, Wearable and Ubiquitous Technologies (IMWUT), Vol. 2, No. 1, Article 1. March 2018. DOI: 10.1145/3161166
Abstract. Reading is a fundamental daily activity, yet millions of individuals with visual impairments face significant barriers to independent access to printed text in real-world environments. FingerReader2.0 is a wearable ring-shaped device worn on the index finger that combines real-time optical character recognition (OCR) with audio feedback, enabling users to read printed text by sliding their finger across lines of text. This paper presents the iterative design and evaluation of FingerReader2.0, documenting the transition from a laboratory prototype to a refined, user-centred assistive device. Through a series of user studies with visually impaired participants, we evaluated reading speed, accuracy, user satisfaction, and the social acceptability of the device in everyday contexts. Key design contributions include an improved form factor, enhanced text tracking algorithms, and a new reading mode that accommodates diverse reading materials including books, product labels, and menus. Results demonstrate significant improvements in reading performance and user acceptance compared to the first-generation device, with participants reporting increased confidence and independence in reading tasks outside the home.

Research context & motivation

Approximately 253 million people worldwide live with visual impairment, with 36 million classified as blind. While screen readers and digital accessibility tools have transformed access to digital content, the challenge of reading physical printed text — menus, labels, signs, handwritten notes — remains a significant barrier to independence.

The FingerReader project, developed at MIT Media Lab and collaborating institutions, addresses this gap with a natural, embodied interaction paradigm: reading by touch and guided audio feedback. The finger-worn form factor aligns with the natural act of following text with one's finger, making the learning curve intuitive.

Design iteration process

FingerReader2.0 represents a substantial design evolution from the original prototype. The research team conducted contextual inquiry with visually impaired participants, analysed failure modes of the first-generation device, and applied iterative prototyping informed by participatory design sessions.

Key improvements addressed three domains: hardware miniaturisation and wearability; OCR pipeline accuracy across diverse font types and paper qualities; and the social dimension of assistive device use in public contexts — an often-neglected but critically important aspect of AT adoption.

Principal investigators

R. Boldu (lead author) and Alexandru Dancu, PhD (co-author, EyeMap.ai Labs CTO) led the design and evaluation phases of the project. The work was conducted in collaboration with MIT Media Lab and affiliated institutions.

Device Features

💍

Ring Form Factor

Worn on the index finger. Minimal physical intrusion allows natural sliding motion across lines of text in any orientation.

🔤

Real-Time OCR

On-device optical character recognition processes text as the user's finger moves, with robust performance across font types and print quality variations.

🔊

Audio Feedback

Synthesised speech output delivers recognised words and navigational cues (line detected, end of line, word boundary) through a connected earpiece.

📍

Tracking Algorithm

Improved text-line tracking algorithm detects deviations from text lines and guides the user back — reducing reading errors without interrupting flow.

Relation to EyeMap.ai Research

The continuum from detection to support

EyeMap.ai Labs positions assistive technology research as a natural complement to diagnostic AI. Early detection of conditions like AMD, DR, and glaucoma — the focus of our AI models — prevents vision loss and reduces the population requiring assistive devices. For those who do experience vision impairment, devices like FingerReader 2.0 represent the translational endpoint of the broader vision-health research mission.

Our principal investigator's cross-disciplinary background — spanning clinical wearables, computer vision, and human-computer interaction — enables EyeMap.ai Labs to maintain credible research programmes across both prevention and rehabilitation dimensions of ophthalmic care.

Future work in this programme will explore integration of large vision-language models into next-generation reading assistance devices, building on the FingerReader platform.
